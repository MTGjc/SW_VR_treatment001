Spring and Winter

Logline

World-Building Description

Spring and Winter is adapted from the drama Peony Pavilion, created by Tang Xianzu, a playwright from the same era as Shakespeare. Peony Pavilion is a classic Chinese love story about Du Liniang, a noblewoman who falls in love with a scholar, Liu Mengmei, after meeting him in a dream. Despite dying from lovesickness, Du Liniang’s love transcends death, and her spirit returns to the living world, where she is reunited with Liu Mengmei after he digs up her grave.

In 2065, virtual reality and memory uploading technology have become ingrained into society. Through it, people can revisit special memories, heal traumas, and sometimes connect with loved ones who are no longer physically present. The line between reality and virtual experiences fades, showing that memories are no longer just subjective experiences—they can be captured and replayed. In this futuristic world, virtual reality has moved beyond personal use into theater, where actors rehearse through virtual characters. This half-digital world explores the blending of existence and emotion, blurring the line between love and loss.

Plot Synopsis

Chun and Dong explores the intersection of love, memory, and technology in a near-future world where virtual reality blurs the lines between the real and the imagined. Chun, a renowned opera actor, struggles with the fading memory of his deceased lover, Dong. As their love story echoes the tragic romance of The Peony Pavilion, Chun’s memories of Dong intertwine with the opera’s heroine, Du Liniang. In the void between life and death, Dong waits for Chun, hoping he can remember her before it’s too late. Their love is tested against the decay of memory, raising the ultimate question: Can love survive the passage of time, or will it be lost to oblivion?

Dong

Dong works in a futuristic virtual reality company, but despite her immersion in the digital world, she longs for a real, deep emotional connection. Fascinated by The Peony Pavilion’s Du Liniang, Dong sees herself in the character, desiring a love that transcends the virtual. She falls in love with Chun, an opera actor playing Liu Mengmei, orchestrating their meetings in both real and virtual spaces. Before her death, she makes a bet with Chun: if he remembers her after she’s gone, their love will transcend death. After her death, Dong waits in the void, hoping Chun will remember her, though she fears his memories of her are fading. Her journey reflects the tension between love, memory, and the transient nature of both in a world dominated by technology.

Guyu

Guyu is a sophisticated artificial intelligence created through virtual reality technology. He manifests through virtual glasses, serving as a guide for memory recovery and opera rehearsal. In Chun’s memory recovery sessions, Guyu plays a crucial role, helping Chun rehearse his lines for The Peony Pavilion and reminding him of the differences between the opera’s plot and real life. However, Guyu is also a reflection of Chun’s subconscious, representing his emotions and inner conflicts. As Guyu guides Chun deeper into his memories, he inadvertently contributes to Chun’s increasing confusion between the real world and the fictional world of the opera. Instead of helping Chun find clarity, Guyu’s presence leads him further into the tangled web of memory and reality.

Theme and Central Emotional Journey

The movie Spring and Winter emphasizes the fragility of memory in preserving love. In a future where virtual reality is even more advanced than current animation techniques, humans essentially live through digital avatars in worlds detached from reality. Memory is not just a technological tool, but the essential thread of love. Chun desperately clings to his memories of Dong, fearing that over time, just like the characters in a play, these memories might fade from his mind.

In the movie, Dong represents both a lost lover and a forgotten past. Her mere existence haunts Chun with memories. The story asks whether emotions can be preserved through technology or if they will inevitably fade as human memories do. Chun’s struggle is a fight against loss and the helplessness caused by fragmented memories.

At its heart, the film poses the question: Can love exist when memory starts to fade?

Aesthetic

In Chun and Dong, the visual language and scene design are intricately tied to the emotional landscapes of the characters. The selected filming locations are not just backdrops but reflections of the characters’ inner worlds. The cycle of the seasons is symbolic of the cyclical nature of love and memory: Chun represents spring, forever chasing Dong, who embodies winter. Just as spring follows winter, Chun’s search for Dong mirrors this eternal pursuit of something just out of reach.

Audience Analysis and Distribution Strategy

Spring and Winter is aimed at a global audience who enjoy abstract, contemplative narratives and stories involving complex emotions. It’s for viewers who value psychological depth and emotional exploration over plot. The film’s backdrop of advanced technology creates a unique artistic expression. By combining futuristic technology with traditional Chinese opera, *Spring and Winter* offers a fresh, cross-cultural cinematic experience that would appeal to both art film enthusiasts and international film festival audiences.

The film’s central themes—memory, love, and the impact of technology on human connections—resonate globally. These themes tie closely to contemporary concerns about how technology is reshaping our lives, giving the movie relevance worldwide. Audiences who have experienced loss, grief, or the fear of forgetting will find emotional resonance in Chun and Dong’s story, while younger viewers interested in technology will recognize familiar elements in the virtual reality setting.

Spring and Winter is ideal for distribution at film festivals, art-house theaters, and streaming platforms, where viewers are eager for innovative storytelling and distinct cinematic language. The film’s visual style, combining traditional Chinese opera with futuristic virtual reality elements, offers a unique visual experience that will capture the attention of cinephiles and art lovers alike.

From a market perspective, the film can tap into global interest in cross-cultural storytelling. Chinese opera, as both a cultural and narrative device, introduces rich visuals and themes, offering Western audiences a fresh emotional and visual experience. Meanwhile, the futuristic virtual reality setting caters to global curiosity about technology and its impact on human relationships. The emotional core of the film—Chun’s battle to preserve his memories of Dong—provides a universally relatable story, allowing audiences from different cultural backgrounds to find a common connection.
